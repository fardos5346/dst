Distance, Speed, and Time

History & Motivation

Since ancient times, tracking movement has been crucial for survival, trade, and astronomy. Early humans needed to estimate travel time between resources. The formalization of these concepts solidified with Galileo Galilei and Sir Isaac Newton in the 17th century, paving the way for classical mechanics.

The Formula Triangle

The magic triangle helps you easily remember how to arrange the formula based on the variable you need to calculate.

Distance (D)
Speed (S)
Time (T)

When to use which?

  • To find Distance (D): Cover D, leaving Speed × Time. Use this when you know how fast and for how long something moved. Formula: D = Speed × Time
  • To find Speed (S): Cover S, leaving Distance / Time. Use this when you know the total ground covered and the time it took. Formula: Speed = Distance / Time
  • To find Time (T): Cover T, leaving Distance / Speed. Use this when you know the distance to travel and your fixed speed rate. Formula: Time = Distance / Speed

Speed Unit Conversion (km/h to m/s)

Often in physics and real-world math, we must convert speed units between kilometers per hour (km/h) and meters per second (m/s). Here is how the math rules work:

  • There are 1000 meters in 1 kilometer, and 3600 seconds in 1 hour (60 min × 60 sec).
  • Therefore: 1 km/h = 1000m / 3600s = 1 / 3.6 m/s
  • The Magic Shortcut Factor (3.6):
    • To convert from km/h to m/s: Divide by 3.6.
      Example: 90 km/h / 3.6 = 25 m/s
    • To convert from m/s to km/h: Multiply by 3.6.
      Example: 10 m/s × 3.6 = 36 km/h
